The Beginning

From Faraskur to Cairo

Born on November 30, 1906, in the city of Faraskur, Damietta, Riad Al Sunbati's destiny was written in musical notes. His father was a singer, and young Riad would often escape school to listen to his father's band play.

When he lost his eyesight temporarily due to a disease at age nine, music became his vision. He mastered the Oud with such proficiency that he was nicknamed "Bulbul Al-Mansoura" (The Nightingale of Mansoura).

Unlike his contemporaries who rushed to modernize by borrowing heavily from Western classical music, Al Sunbati remained the guardian of the Eastern identity. He modernized from within, evolving the Taqsim and the Qasida while respecting the Maqam.

The Reluctant Star

The Golden Voice

Before he was the "Genius of Melody," Riad Al Sunbati was known as "Bulbul Al-Mansoura" (The Nightingale of Mansoura). Possessing a warm, emotive voice, he started his career singing in the 1920s.

Despite his immense vocal talent, he deliberately chose to prioritize composing, believing his true legacy lay in creating music for others. However, he left us rare vocal gems that showcase his mastery of the Oud and his deep emotional capacity.

He starred in only one film, Habeeb Qalbi (1952), where he sang masterpieces like "Ala Oudi". In his later years, he recorded personal masterpieces like "Ashwaq" that remain references for Oud playing and singing.

The Golden Partnership

Sunbati & The Lady

01

The Guardian of the Poem

While other composers gave Umm Kulthum modern, lighter songs, Sunbati was entrusted with the heavy, classical Arabic poems (*Qasidas*). He had the unique ability to make difficult Arabic grammar sound melodic and emotional.

Al-Atlal

الأطلال (The Ruins)

Considered by many critics as the "Crown of Arabic Song." Released in 1966, this masterpiece cemented their legacy. It combines power, nostalgia, and intricate musical phrasing that drives audiences into ecstasy (Tarab).

02

200+ Songs

He composed more songs for Umm Kulthum than any other composer. Their journey spanned decades, from the 1930s "Ala Balad El-Mahboub" to the spiritual "Thawrat Al-Shak" and religious masterpieces like "Nahj El-Burda".
